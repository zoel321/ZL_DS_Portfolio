What I Learned About Myself While Studying Data Science

February 25, 2022

Here’s what I’ve learned about myself during the bootcamp:

1. I cannot be a perfectionist.

I quickly learned you can’t predict what you kind of problems you will run into, especially as a beginner.

When I first started the bootcamp, I wanted to have a clear idea of how long things would take and be able to plan my schedule/project approach accordingly.

However, I soon learned that data science is an iterative process, and unpredictable. You don’t know what the data is going to tell you ahead of time–you have to explore it, and that requires patience (and intuition that can be developed). It’s also impossible to know how long something will take, especially if you have never done it before.

Though uncomfortable for perfectionists, it’s possible to do things while still learning! In Metis, we have to submit a project proposal during the first week of each module. For the first few modules, as I wrote my proposal, I would think “How can I plan to do this…when I don’t know what any of these tools/methods are or how they work…”

I eventually got comfortable (at least a little more comfortable…) with the uncertainty and trusted that I didn’t have to know the full picture to just get started.

2. This is just the beginning. Which isn’t a bad thing.

The more you start learning something, the more you realize you don’t know anything.

Bootcamps are short and I can’t expect to come out of one being fully knowledgeable. Even after 6 years of studying pharmacy, I can’t tell you what every medication does or the underlying mechanism. Data science is an even broader field, with its foundations in many areas–math, computer science, business.

I think that figuring out what you don’t know is a great insight in itself. The skills learned through a bootcamp (or even college/grad school) are all the initial tools in a toolbox. Every lesson learned and project done is not going to complete the toolbox (nor would I expect it to), but it doesn’t need to–as long as I’m adding to the collection, that’s something I can be proud of.

3. Find a way to measure personal growth.

We all know comparison is the thief of joy but it can still creep into us without awareness. After finishing my first data science project, instead of feeling joy, I felt inadequate.

“Who would take me seriously with this analysis? How will I ever be able to get a job?” I would wonder as I saw other students' projects, or after just perusing online for portfolio examples.

I try to remind myself of this advice: “the only person you should compare yourself to is the person you were yesterday.” As long as I’m learning or improving, that’s what matters to me. And to remind myself of this, any time I feel discouraged, I’ll think back to my older work/projects and reflect on how I’ve improved my data skills since then.

4. Experience/practice makes all things better.

I don’t think it’s a coincidence that the initial period of anything feels super slow. The first week of school, first month of a new job, first year in a new city…time moves slowly and there’s an adjustment period. I don’t know why I was surprised by this, but I felt the same for the first 2 modules of the bootcamp.

Then suddenly everything started flying by! I started getting into a routine, figuring out how I learned best in a remote setting, slowly understanding how long certain tasks take (again, not perfect, but a loose idea).

When new things are hard or take a long time, I always seem to forget that “the beginning” means…just that. A starting point, a transitional phase.

5. Live in the moment.

It’s easy for me to get caught up in the end goal. The “when I…(finish this project)… then I… (will feel happy/relaxed. can take a break.)” But that’s not living. When things are fast-paced, it’s easy for me to get caught up in the doing and less in the being. When I take a step back, I always feel really lucky to have the time and energy to pursue this career pivot.
